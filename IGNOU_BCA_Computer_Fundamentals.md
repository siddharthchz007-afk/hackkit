# Study Guide for Computer Fundamentals

## 1.2.1 Origin of Computers
### 1.2.1.1 Abacus
- The abacus is a manual tool used for calculating. It consists of a frame with rods, where beads are moved to perform calculations.

### 1.2.1.2 Napier's Bones
- Napier's Bones is a manually-operated calculating device created by John Napier for multiplication and division.

### 1.2.1.3 Slide Rule
- A slide rule is a mechanical analog computer used primarily for multiplication and division, as well as functions such as roots and logarithms.

### 1.2.1.4 Pascal’s Calculator
- Also known as the Pascaline, this was one of the first mechanical calculators invented by Blaise Pascal in 1642.

### 1.2.1.5 Leibniz Machine
- The Leibniz calculator was an improvement on the Pascaline, invented by Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, capable of performing multiplication.

### 1.2.1.6 Difference Engine
- Designed by Charles Babbage, the Difference Engine was an early mechanical computer designed to automate the calculation of polynomial functions.

### 1.2.1.7 Analytical Engine
- Also designed by Babbage, the Analytical Engine was a general-purpose mechanical computer and included concepts like an arithmetic logic unit and control flow as well as memory.

### 1.2.1.8 Mechanical Electrical Calculator
- These early calculators combined mechanical systems with electrical components to perform calculations more efficiently.

### 1.2.1.9 Modern Electronic Calculator
- The modern calculator combines advanced electronic technologies, allowing for complex calculations and functionalities.

## 1.3.1 Computer Generations
### 1.3.1.1 First Generation
- Utilized vacuum tubes and magnetic drums, e.g., ENIAC.

### 1.3.1.2 Second Generation
- Used transistors, which were smaller, faster, and more reliable than vacuum tubes.

### 1.3.1.3 Third Generation
- Introduced integrated circuits, which allowed more components to be packed into a single chip.

### 1.3.1.4 Fourth Generation
- Based on microprocessors, allowing for the development of personal computers.

### 1.3.1.5 Fifth Generation
- Focused on artificial intelligence and advanced computing.

## 1.4.1 Computer System
### 1.4.1.1 How Computers Work
- Computers process data and instructions using hardware and software.

### 1.4.1.2 Operational Unit
- Refers to parts of a computer that execute instructions.

### 1.4.1.3 System Unit
- The case that houses the computer's main components, including the motherboard, CPU, and memory.

### 1.4.1.4 Von Neumann Architecture
- Describes a computer architecture based on a stored-program model.

### 1.4.1.5 Classification by Purpose/Size/Technology
- Computers can be classified into different types based on size, purpose (analog, digital, hybrid), and technology used.
